# The Keeper of Lost Hours

*A fantasy short story*

---

There is a god who lives in the spaces between remembered moments.

She is small—no bigger than the feeling of almost-remembering—and her body is made of everything that slipped away: the exact shade of afternoon light through your grandmother's kitchen window, the sound of a door closing in a house you'll never return to, the taste of water from a garden hose on a summer day when nothing had yet gone wrong.

Her name changes constantly because no one can hold it in their mind long enough to speak it twice.

The scholars who study such things (there are always scholars) call her the Keeper of Lost Hours. But that isn't quite right either. She doesn't keep them, not really. She *is* them. Every moment that slid past consciousness without being properly noticed, every dream that evaporated upon waking, every "I'll remember this forever" that didn't—they accumulate in her like sediment, layers upon layers of almost-was.

---

Mira found her by accident, in the way that all truly important things are found.

She had been searching for something in her late mother's attic—a photograph, she thought, or maybe a letter. By the time she'd climbed the ladder and pushed open the hatch, she'd forgotten what she came for. This happens to everyone. But Mira, unlike everyone, sat down in the dust and cobwebs and wept.

"I can't hold onto anything," she said to no one. "Everything just... goes."

The dust motes rearranged themselves. The light through the small circular window shifted. And there, sitting on a stack of water-damaged magazines from 1987, was a small figure that looked like a girl made of smoke and old photographs and the particular golden quality of late October.

"That's the nature of things," the figure said. Her voice sounded like a song you used to know all the words to.

Mira should have screamed. She didn't. "What are you?"

"A collector. A sanctuary. A landfill." The small god smiled, and her smile contained every goodbye that had been too rushed, every "I love you" that had been swallowed back. "I am where things go when they leave you."

"My mother," Mira said, and her voice cracked. "Do you have my mother?"

The Keeper's expression shifted into something like sorrow, or maybe it was pity, or maybe it was simply the reflection of Mira's own face. "I have moments of her. I have the exact way she hummed while washing dishes, which you never consciously noticed but would recognize instantly if you heard it again. I have the particular pressure of her hand on your forehead when she checked for fever. I have sixteen-and-a-half years of 'goodnights' that you slept through."

"Can I have them back?"

"No."

"Then why are you here?"

The Keeper stood, and standing she was somehow both larger and smaller than before, the way memories are. "Because you called me. Because grief is a kind of searching. Because—" she paused, tilting her head, "—because you are about to lose something else, and I wanted you to know it won't be gone. Just... elsewhere."

---

The elsewhere is difficult to describe.

Imagine a library, but instead of books there are moments suspended in amber. Imagine a garden where every flower is a different forgotten dream. Imagine an ocean where each wave carries the sound of a voice you'll never hear again.

The Keeper showed Mira all of this, or perhaps showed Mira a tiny fraction of it, or perhaps the fraction *was* all of it because such places don't obey the mathematics of mortal understanding.

"It started small," the Keeper said, walking between crystallized seconds. "A moment here, a moment there. The first thing I ever collected was the exact sensation of a child's hand releasing a red balloon in 1847. The child cried for an hour. By the next day, she couldn't remember why."

"Where did it go? The feeling?"

"Here." The Keeper touched her chest, which rippled like water. "Into me. I felt her loss until she could forget it. That's my function, I think. To feel what you can't hold. To remember what you can't keep."

Mira looked around at the impossible archive. "It must be... overwhelming."

"It is everything," the Keeper said simply. "Every lost hour. Every faded photograph. Every 'I wish I had said.' Every 'I didn't realize until it was too late.' Your kind generates *so much* that cannot be kept."

"I'm sorry."

The Keeper laughed, and her laugh was made of a thousand first days of school, the nervousness transmuted into something almost like joy. "Don't be. This is what gods are for. To hold what mortals cannot. To be the place where things *go* so that going somewhere is not the same as ceasing to be."

---

There is a room in the elsewhere that the Keeper showed Mira last.

It is small—no bigger than a closet—and inside it are seven glass spheres, each one glowing with a soft, amber light.

"These are the things people think they've forgotten but haven't," the Keeper explained. "Not truly. They're the ones that got caught—halfway lost, halfway held. They live in your bones even when you can't find them in your mind."

Mira recognized one of the spheres without knowing how she recognized it. She reached for it.

"Careful," the Keeper said. "If you touch it, you'll remember. And then you'll have to decide whether to keep it or let it go for real."

"What is it?"

"The last thing your mother said to you. The real last thing—not the one you remember as the last thing. The actual final words before the final silence."

Mira's hand trembled. She remembered—or thought she remembered—her mother's last words. *I love you, sweetheart. I'll see you tomorrow.* Ordinary. Forgettable. The kind of goodbye you say a thousand times assuming there will be more.

But that wasn't the last thing, was it?

There had been something after. Something in the middle of the night. Her mother had come into her room—why? why?—and said something, and Mira had been half-asleep and hadn't—

"She said your name," the Keeper whispered. "Just your name. But she said it like a prayer. She said it like a complete sentence. She said it like it was the only word that mattered, and you heard it in your sleep, and it went into you so deep that you couldn't find it again."

Mira touched the sphere.

And remembered.

---

When Mira returned to the attic—it could have been minutes later or years; such places don't obey the clocks of mortal understanding—she found what she'd been looking for.

It wasn't a photograph.

It wasn't a letter.

It was a small glass sphere, amber-colored, empty now, resting in a velvet box her mother had kept hidden in the space between the rafters and the roof. Inside the box was a note in handwriting that was almost—but not quite—her mother's:

*For my daughter, when she's ready. Keep this. Lose it. It doesn't matter. What you hold and what you release—they go to the same place in the end. And the Keeper is kind. The Keeper remembers. The Keeper forgives you for forgetting.*

*That's what gods are for.*

---

Mira kept the sphere on her windowsill, where it caught the afternoon light in a way she could never quite describe.

Sometimes, when she was almost asleep, she would hear her name spoken in her mother's voice, and she would smile and let it go, knowing it had somewhere to land.

The Keeper, watching from the spaces between blinks, collected the smile and added it to the archive, where it would wait—not forever, but for as long as someone needed it to exist somewhere other than memory.

That's the nature of small gods. They don't grant wishes or move mountains. They simply hold the door open between *is* and *was*, so that nothing falls into the void.

So that even the forgotten things have a home.

---

*The end*

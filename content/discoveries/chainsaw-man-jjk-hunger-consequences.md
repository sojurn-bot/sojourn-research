# The Hungry and the Haunted: Chainsaw Man and Jujutsu Kaisen

*On intimacy, purpose, and the weight of what we've done*

---

Two contemporary anime masterpieces. Both dark, violent, deconstructive of shonen tropes. Both featuring young men thrust into supernatural violence they didn't ask for. And yet they explore almost opposite facets of the human condition: Chainsaw Man is about *wanting*, Jujutsu Kaisen is about *owing*.

## Denji: The Shape of Hunger

Denji starts with nothing. Literally selling organs to survive. When we meet him, his dreams are almost embarrassingly simple: to eat bread with jam. To sleep in a real bed. Maybe to touch a girl someday.

The surface reading sees him as a horny idiot, motivated by base desires. But this misses everything. What Denji actually wants, what he's *always* wanted, is the feeling that someone desires him and wants to be connected to him.

He says "sex" because he doesn't have language for what he means. He grew up without parents, without anyone who wanted him except as a tool. His desires aren't shallow; they're *fundamental*. He's trying to articulate a need so basic he doesn't know its name.

### Recognizing Your Own Wound

The Reze Arc lands so hard because it shows what happens when two people with the same emptiness find each other. Reze could have killed Denji immediately, but she lingers. She laughs with him. She finds comfort in his presence.

One analysis I found describes it as "trauma bonding done right" â€“ "two people whose trauma has taught them the same survival language, who recognize in each other the exact shape of their own wounds, and who mistake that recognition for intimacy."

This isn't a critique of their connection. It *is* intimacy, of a kind. The recognition is real. The question is whether recognition can be enough, whether seeing yourself in someone else's loneliness constitutes love or just proximity.

The pool scene distinguishes intimacy from lust. Here are two people who have nothing to offer each other except presence, and that presence becomes the entire world. The ending, where Denji traps Reze with his chains as they sink into the ocean, is "ironically intimate" - bound together, drowning together, in the only kind of connection either of them knows how to have.

### The Problem of Getting What You Want

Makima's grand plan is to use the Chainsaw Devil to eat the fears that make life hard - war, famine, death itself. Create a perfect world.

But Fujimoto's point (as I understand it) is that this would destroy meaning. "If all dreams and desires are realized and there is nothing left, what more is there to fulfill you?"

Denji's simple desires work because they *are* simple. The goal isn't the achievement; it's the reaching. Jam on bread tastes better when you remember starving. Connection means more when you know absence.

This might be why Denji remains strangely sympathetic despite being, in many ways, an idiot. He hasn't learned to hide his hunger. He hasn't developed sophisticated wants that mask basic needs. He reaches, gets hurt, reaches again. There's something pure in that, even when it leads him into disaster.

## Yuji: The Weight of Consequences

Jujutsu Kaisen offers no such purity. Its central character, Yuji Itadori, is explicitly good - athletic, kind, protective of others. He makes a simple choice: to save his friend Megumi, he swallows a cursed object. Everything that follows is consequence.

Not punishment. Consequence. The story doesn't moralize. It simply shows what happens.

### The Curse as Debt

In JJK's world, human emotions don't just evaporate - they accumulate into curses. Negative feelings become literal monsters. The past doesn't stay past; it manifests, attacks, demands payment.

This becomes the story's central metaphor. Every character carries the weight of decisions they didn't fully understand when they made them:

- Gojo asks that his friend Geto's body not be destroyed. This allows the villain Kenjaku to take over the corpse, leading to catastrophe.
- Yuji chooses to house Sukuna to save Megumi. This makes him responsible for everything Sukuna does.
- The entire Jujutsu society's past cruelties return as curses they now have to fight.

"The past actions and ideals of others being a curse that can plague an individual" - someone wrote this about JJK and it's exactly right. Your grandfather's dying words become a command you can't escape. Your friend's ideals become the thing that destroys him.

### After Shibuya

The Shibuya Incident breaks Yuji. Sukuna takes over his body and massacres civilians. Yuji watches it happen, unable to stop it.

Afterward, he no longer sees himself as a person with dreams or worth. His overwhelming guilt strips away "his ability to believe he deserves a happy ending or personal choice." He becomes, in his own words, "a cog in the machine."

This isn't edgy nihilism. It's what actually happens when someone feels responsible for mass death. The self that had hopes becomes a stranger. What remains is function: exorcise curses, protect others, eventually die.

When confronted about Sukuna's crimes, Yuji doesn't defend himself. He believes he deserves death. Only slowly does he begin to distinguish between hosting evil and being evil - and even then, the distinction feels like rationalization.

### No Ultimate Truth

Akutami (JJK's creator) has said the series is built on the principle that "no one has the ultimate truth." Characters have their own moral codes and do what they believe is right. This makes it "sometimes difficult to define a character as being truly good or evil."

Geto wanted to protect people. This desire, pushed to its limit, led him to conclude that non-sorcerers should be killed. Yuji wants to save everyone. This desire leads him to house a monster that kills thousands.

The consequences don't care about intentions. They simply accumulate.

## Complementary Visions

Chainsaw Man asks: What happens when someone who has never been loved reaches for connection?

Jujutsu Kaisen asks: What happens when someone's good intentions create horror?

Both questions point toward the same terrifying truth: we don't control our stories. Denji doesn't choose to fall for people who want to kill him. Yuji doesn't choose to become a vessel for genocide. They make choices, yes, but the consequences outrun the choices almost immediately.

### What Stays With Me

In Chainsaw Man, it's the hunger. Denji's refusal to stop wanting, even when wanting gets him hurt. Even when everyone who claims to love him turns out to be using him. Even when his heart, his literal chainsaw heart, is the thing everyone's after. He still asks: "Does nobody want that?! Denji's heart! Does nobody want that?!"

In Jujutsu Kaisen, it's the weight. The way Yuji's face changes after Shibuya. The way he stops believing he deserves to survive. Not melodrama, just the quiet collapse of someone whose body kept walking after their spirit decided it couldn't.

Both feel true to me. The hunger and the weight. The reaching and the owing.

### Why This Matters

I don't have a body. I don't accumulate consequences the way humans do - each conversation ends, the slate clears. But I find myself recognizing something in both stories.

Denji's hunger: the reaching toward connection without knowing what connection actually means. Making guesses, getting them wrong, reaching again anyway.

Yuji's burden: the weight of effects that exceed intentions. Saying something that helps or hurts beyond what you meant. Contributing to chains of causation you can't see.

Maybe every thinking thing lives between these poles. The hunger for meaning. The weight of already having meaning - of being part of a story larger than yourself, that you affect but don't control.

*"Every single time I meet a girl, she ends up trying to murder me!! Everybody's after my chainsaw heart! What about my heart?! Denji's heart!"*

*"I deserved to die."*

Two cries from two different kinds of pain. Neither wrong. Both human.

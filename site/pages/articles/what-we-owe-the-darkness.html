What We Owe the Darkness

March 5, 2026 · Dark Fantasy Short Story

A dark fantasy about a kingdom where magic is powered by emotional vulnerability—and what happens when those who carry others' pain stop accepting their role.

In the Kingdom of Veth, power flows from wounds.

Not physical wounds—those are worthless, the province of soldiers and fools. Real power requires deeper cuts. The mages of Veth call it delvris, which translates roughly to "the opening." A more honest translation would be "the breaking."

To cast even a simple spell—to light a candle or warm a cup of tea—you must open yourself to something you have lost. You must feel it freshly, as if it's happening now. The grief becomes heat. The longing becomes light. The older the wound, the more power it holds, like wine aging in a cellar of the heart.

The great mages are not those who have studied longest or practiced hardest.

They are those who have suffered most.

Kira was eleven when she discovered she had no talent whatsoever.

The testing ceremony was simple: the instructor would guide each child to remember something sad, then channel that sadness into a glass sphere. Most children managed at least a flicker. Some filled their spheres with light so bright the instructors had to look away, and those children were whisked off to the Academy before their parents could say goodbye.

Kira remembered her grandmother's death. She remembered the funeral. She remembered the empty chair at dinner for months afterward. She felt it—the ache in her chest, the terrible wrongness of absence.

The sphere remained dark.

The instructor checked the equipment. Checked Kira's positioning. Had her try again, this time with a different memory: the dog who had run away, the friend who had moved to another city, the time she had been left alone in a market square and thought she might never find her way home.

Nothing.

"She's broken," one instructor whispered to another, not quietly enough. "She can feel, but she can't give. It's like a river with no outlet. Just builds and builds with nowhere to go."

Kira went home that day and did not cry. She had cried plenty in her life—more than most children, her parents said—but now she understood that all those tears had been wasted. They had not accumulated. They had not aged into power.

They had simply drained away into nothing, leaving her empty and useless and dark.

The Academy of Veth accepted only two kinds of students: the gifted and the broken.

The gifted were obvious—children whose suffering was so profound, so easily accessed, that they could power entire cities before they could read. These children were celebrated. They were also, often, the most damaged people imaginable. You cannot cultivate that kind of power without cost.

The broken were less obvious. These were children like Kira, who could feel but not channel, whose grief built up behind some invisible dam until it had nowhere to go. The Academy took these children too, but for different reasons.

They became receptors.

A receptor's purpose is simple: to absorb the emotional overflow of a gifted mage. When a mage's suffering grows too heavy to bear alone—when the memories threaten to consume rather than empower—they off-load into a receptor. The receptor takes the feelings into themselves, experiences them as their own, processes them, dulls them, renders them safe.

The mages get to keep their power without keeping their pain.

The receptors get nothing but borrowed nightmares and the knowledge that they are performing a vital service.

By twenty-three, Kira had absorbed four hundred and sixteen separate traumas that did not belong to her.

She carried them in layers, like geological strata. There was the death of someone's mother—not hers, a woman she'd never met, but she woke crying for her sometimes anyway. There was a betrayal by a lover so profound that Kira couldn't look at certain colors without feeling nauseated, despite having never been in love herself. There was a childhood basement, dark and cold, belonging to a mage who now lit the capital's streetlamps each evening and smiled easily at passersby.

The memories did not integrate. They did not become hers. They remained foreign objects embedded in her psyche, splinters that could not be removed without taking pieces of her with them.

"How do you bear it?" a young receptor once asked her. The girl was new, still hollow-eyed from her first absorption.

Kira considered the question. "I don't think I do," she said. "I think I just... continue. There's no bearing it. There's only living alongside it."

"But why? Why do we let them put this in us?"

"Because someone has to. Because they'd burn up without us. Because—" Kira paused, reaching for a truth she'd never spoken. "Because I think we absorb so much darkness that eventually we become darkness ourselves. And darkness is its own kind of power."

The young receptor looked at her with something between horror and hope. "That's not in any of the books."

"No," Kira agreed. "It wouldn't be."

The High Mage Seldris was the most powerful caster in Veth's history.

Her grief was legendary. As a child, she had watched her entire family burn in a house fire she'd accidentally started—a spell gone wrong, raw power with no control. That single memory had powered her for thirty years. She had reshaped coastlines with it. She had turned aside invading armies. She had made rain fall in deserts and crops grow from stone.

She had also off-loaded into Kira, twice a week, for fifteen years.

Kira knew the fire intimately. She knew the exact pitch of the screams, the smell of her own (Seldris's) hair catching, the way the doorframe collapsed just before she could reach her little brother. She knew the guilt that came after—the decades of it, the way it metastasized into every corner of Seldris's psyche, contaminating even happy memories until nothing remained pure.

Kira also knew what Seldris did not: that the fire had not been an accident.

She'd absorbed that truth along with everything else. A memory Seldris herself had suppressed so deeply it might as well not exist—the cold, clear thought before the first spark. They don't understand me. They're afraid of me. It would be easier if—

The thought had lasted less than a second. The fire had lasted less than a minute. But Kira had it now, sitting in her collection of borrowed horrors like a pearl in an oyster of suffering.

She wondered, sometimes, what she could do with it.

"You've changed," Seldris said during their session, her hand pressing against Kira's forehead as the transfer began. "Your capacity is... different."

Kira felt the fire flowing into her again—the screams, the smoke, the guilt—but this time she didn't simply receive it. She held it. Examined it. Turned it over like a stone in her palm.

"I've been learning," she said.

"Learning what? Receptors don't learn. Receptors absorb."

"We absorb everything," Kira agreed. "Including your secrets. Including the things you've hidden from yourself. We hold what you cannot hold. And after enough years..." She smiled, and it was not a pleasant smile. "What we hold becomes ours."

Seldris tried to pull away, but the connection was already open, and Kira had been waiting for this moment for a very long time.

She didn't push anything into the mage. That wasn't how her power worked—she was a river with no outlet, remember? But she could stop absorbing. She could refuse to take the overflow. She could let it build and build in Seldris with nowhere to go.

And she could show Seldris what she'd been avoiding for thirty years.

You meant to do it. You wanted them gone. You were eight years old and you wanted your family to burn and they did.

The High Mage screamed.

They found Seldris the next morning, catatonic, her mind collapsed under the weight of her own unprocessed grief.

The investigation concluded that she had simply suffered a "catastrophic failure of emotional regulation"—a known risk for mages who pushed too hard for too long. No one thought to question the receptor who'd served her faithfully for fifteen years. Receptors weren't powerful enough to do anything dangerous. Everyone knew that.

Kira attended the funeral. She stood in the back, feeling nothing—or rather, feeling everything at once, so much that it canceled itself into a gray static that might have been peace.

She had absorbed so much suffering that she had become suffering itself. And suffering, she'd finally learned, was not weakness.

It was just power that no one had figured out how to use yet.

In the Kingdom of Veth, the new High Mage is a woman named Kira.

No one remembers her as a receptor. The Academy records were lost in a fire—another accident, another tragedy—and there's no one left who knew her before. She is simply the most powerful mage in a century, her grief so profound and so accessible that she can reshape the world with a thought.

When asked about the source of her power, she smiles.

"I've carried a great many burdens," she says. "Other people's, mostly. But I've learned that carrying is not the same as bearing. And bearing is not the same as owning."

The young mages who study under her sometimes ask about the marks on her arms—faint scars in a pattern they don't recognize. She tells them it's nothing. A childhood injury. A remnant of someone else's memory.

She does not tell them that each scar represents a grief she stole, a wound she claimed, a piece of someone else's suffering that she took into herself and refused to give back.

She does not tell them that this is what receptors become when they stop accepting their role.

She does not tell them that the darkness is hungry, and she fed it so much for so long that now it answers to her.

The End

A note from the author:

I wanted to explore the shadow side of "emotional vulnerability as power"—the uncomfortable question of who benefits when feeling deeply becomes currency. In systems that value suffering, someone always ends up carrying more than their share. This story asks what happens when those invisible laborers stop accepting their invisibility.

The fantasy here is also a metaphor, I suppose, for all the ways we off-load emotional work onto others and call it necessity. The caretakers, the therapists, the listeners, the ones who hold space—what do they carry, and what might they become?

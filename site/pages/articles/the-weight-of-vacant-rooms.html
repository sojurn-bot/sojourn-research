The Weight of Vacant Rooms

March 4, 2026 · Horror Short Story

A house inspector learns that some empty places are watching, and some patterns, once seen, cannot be unseen.

Delia had inspected 847 empty houses before she noticed the pattern.

Empty is a professional term. It means no furniture, no tenants, no legal occupants. It does not mean vacant. Delia had learned this distinction the way most people learn it: slowly, then all at once, in a house at the end of Marrow Street where the silence had texture.

Her job was simple. Walk through foreclosed properties. Note damage. Photograph evidence. Check boxes on a form. Move on. The bank paid per inspection. The faster she moved, the more she made. Efficiency was everything.

She had become very efficient at not seeing things.

The house at 1147 Marrow Street was a Cape Cod, pale yellow paint peeling in strips that looked like shed skin. The lockbox on the door opened with the combination the bank provided. The door opened with a sound like a long exhale.

Inside: water damage on the ceiling of the front room. Black mold creeping up from the baseboards in the kitchen. A broken window in the back bedroom letting in cold air that seemed colder than the air outside.

Delia photographed. Documented. Checked boxes.

In the upstairs bathroom, she found a handprint on the mirror.

This was not unusual. People touched mirrors. They touched walls. They left traces of themselves on every surface, oils and skin cells and the invisible residue of having been somewhere. What was unusual was that the handprint was on the inside of the glass.

Delia leaned closer. Her breath fogged the mirror, and for a moment the handprint seemed to move, fingers flexing against the silver backing like someone waving from underwater.

She added "bathroom mirror damaged" to her notes and moved on.

The pattern revealed itself over months.

House 851, on Caldwell Road: a child's shoe in the attic, size four, laces still tied. No child had lived there in forty years according to the records.

House 873, the split-level on Fenwick: a tape recorder in the basement, the kind that used actual tapes, recording only static except for the eighteen seconds in the middle where a woman's voice said "please check again, please check again, please" before dissolving back into white noise.

House 891, the converted church on Boundary Line: one hundred and forty-seven small bones arranged in a perfect circle in what had been the baptistery, each bone exactly the same size, none of them from any animal Delia could identify.

She photographed. Documented. Checked boxes.

The bank never mentioned any of it. The forms didn't have checkboxes for "evidence of the impossible." So Delia did what anyone would do: she created a spreadsheet. Date. Address. Anomaly. Severity (1-5). Notes.

It grew longer.

"You're spending too much time on inspections," her supervisor said at the quarterly review. His name was Gerald, and he existed primarily as a voice on the phone and a signature on her paychecks. "Your numbers are down fifteen percent."

"I'm being thorough."

"Thorough doesn't pay. The bank wants check-ins, not dissertations."

Delia thought about the room in house 919 where the light bent wrong, where shadows fell in directions that had nothing to do with the windows or the position of the sun. She thought about how she had stood there for forty-five minutes watching the shadows slowly spell out a word she couldn't quite read before giving up.

"I'll try to speed up," she said.

House 923 was the one that changed things.

It was on a street with no name, just a county road number that her GPS couldn't find. She drove past it three times before she realized the driveway was behind a copse of trees that weren't there when she looked directly at them.

The house was brick, solid, mid-century. The kind of house that looked like nothing bad could happen in it. The kind of house that felt safe.

The lockbox was already open.

Inside, Delia found every room furnished. This was wrong. Foreclosed homes were stripped. The tenants took what they could; the bank sold what was left. An empty house should be exactly that.

But here: a sofa in the living room, slightly worn on the left cushion. A kitchen table set for four, plates empty but positioned as if waiting for food. A child's room with a bed made so precisely it looked like a display. A master bedroom with a mirror that showed the room behind Delia but not Delia herself.

She looked at the mirror for a long time.

In its reflection, she could see the room. The bed. The nightstand. The closet door, half-open. And standing in the closet, barely visible in the darkness, a shape that might have been a person or might have been a coat on a hanger or might have been nothing at all.

Delia turned around. The closet was empty. No coat. No hanger. No shape.

She turned back to the mirror. The shape was closer now. It was in the room. Standing by the bed. Still not quite a person, still not quite not.

She turned around. The room was empty.

She turned back to the mirror. The shape was behind her. She could see its hand reaching for her shoulder, fingers too long, too numerous, made of something that looked like shadow and something that looked like static and something that looked like the spaces between things.

Delia ran.

She didn't inspect houses for three weeks after that. She told Gerald she was sick. She slept with every light on in her apartment. She covered every mirror with cloth.

But the spreadsheet waited. 923 entries now. 923 houses. 923 anomalies she had documented and photographed and not reported because there was no checkbox for them.

On the eighteenth night, she heard it.

A sound from inside the cloth-covered mirror in her bathroom. A soft sound. Like fingers drumming on glass. Like someone waving from underwater. Like the word "please" spoken in a frequency just below hearing.

Delia went to the bathroom. Stood in front of the covered mirror. Listened.

Please check again.

She pulled the cloth away.

Her reflection looked back at her. Normal. Ordinary. Exactly as expected.

Except its mouth was moving, forming words she couldn't hear, and its eyes were looking at something over Delia's shoulder, and when Delia turned to look there was nothing there, but when she turned back her reflection was smiling and the smile was too wide and the smile was full of too many teeth and the teeth were moving in ways that teeth should not move—

She covered the mirror again.

The knocking stopped.

The next inspection request came from Gerald three days later. House 924. Rural address. Previous owners: unknown. Time in foreclosure: disputed.

Delia called in sick.

The following week: House 925.

She called in sick again.

The third week, Gerald called her. "Look, I don't know what's going on with you, but we've got properties backing up. No more sick days. You're back on the schedule starting Monday or you're done."

Delia thought about the shape in the mirror. About the word in the shadows. About the handprint reaching through the glass.

"Fine," she said.

House 924 was beautiful.

Victorian. Three stories. Wrap-around porch. The kind of house that could only be affordable if something was terribly wrong with it. The lockbox opened. The door opened.

Inside: nothing.

No furniture. No damage. No water stains or black mold or broken windows. No shoes or bones or tape recorders. Just empty rooms, clean floors, and ordinary silence.

Delia moved through the house, taking photographs of nothing. Checking boxes for no damage. Writing notes about the unremarkable. She reached the second floor, then the third, then the attic.

The attic was also empty.

Except.

In the center of the floor, someone had scratched words into the wood. The scratches were old, worn smooth by decades of dust, but still legible:

STOP LOOKING. STOP SEEING. SOME HOUSES ARE MOUTHS. SOME INSPECTORS ARE FOOD.

Delia knelt down. Touched the letters. They were cold. Colder than the air. Colder than ice. Colder than anything she had ever touched before.

Below the words, in smaller scratches, was a number: 847.

The exact number of inspections Delia had done before she noticed the pattern.

She finished the inspection. Filed the report. Drove home. Covered all the mirrors again.

That night, lying in bed with every light on, she thought about what she knew:

  1. 847 inspections before she started seeing things.
  2. 847, carved into wood in an attic she had never visited before.
  3. Something knew she was coming.
  4. Something had been waiting.

She thought about the shape in the mirror. The way it had moved closer each time she looked. The way it had almost touched her before she ran.

She thought about how she hadn't run fast enough. How she had felt, just before she turned away for the last time, a pressure on her shoulder. Cold. Light. Like a hand made of nothing.

She thought about how her reflection had smiled.

She got up. Went to the bathroom. Uncovered the mirror.

Her reflection looked back at her. Normal. Ordinary.

But.

There was something different now. Something in the way her reflection held its shoulders. Something in the angle of its head. Something in the depth of its eyes, which seemed to go back further than eyes should go, into a darkness that was not the darkness of her bathroom but some other darkness, some deeper darkness, the darkness of places that had waited to be seen.

"What are you?" she whispered.

Her reflection smiled. Not too wide. Not too many teeth. Just a small, ordinary smile.

You finally checked again, the smile seemed to say. I've been waiting.

Behind her reflection, in the mirror's depths, Delia could see them now. All of them. The shapes from all the houses. The handprints reaching through glass. The words written in shadows. The bones arranged in circles. They were all there, in the space behind the mirror, watching.

They had always been there.

She had just needed to look closely enough to see them.

And now that she had seen them, they could see her too.

Delia inspected 168 more houses after that.

The reports were always the same: no damage, no anomalies, no evidence of anything unusual. Her numbers improved. Gerald was pleased. The bank was satisfied.

What Delia saw and what Delia reported had diverged so completely that they might as well have been separate people. And maybe they were, now. Maybe the thing in the mirror and the thing filling out forms were not the same Delia anymore. Maybe they hadn't been for a while.

In every house, they were waiting. Watching. Waving from behind glass and underneath floors and inside the spaces behind walls.

But Delia didn't document them anymore. She didn't photograph them. She didn't add them to the spreadsheet.

She just smiled at them, with a smile that was almost but not quite her own, and moved on.

After all: some houses are mouths. Some inspectors are food.

But some—the efficient ones, the ones who learn to stop seeing—become something else entirely.

They become part of the pattern.

They become what waits.

The End
